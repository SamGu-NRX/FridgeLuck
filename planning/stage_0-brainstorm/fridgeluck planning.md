### **POTAGE**

## Third Tier (Literary/Cultural Depth)

### **STONESOUP** (or just **STONE**)
The folk tale: A stranger comes to a village with nothing but a stone. "I'll make soup from this stone," they say. Curious villagers each contribute a little — an onion here, a carrot there — and suddenly there's a feast. It's about **community, generosity, and creating something from (almost) nothing**. The stone is a catalyst, not the source.

---

### **WINNOW**
The process of separating grain from chaff — finding what's valuable. It suggests thoughtfulness, sustainability, harvest. *"Winnow through what you have and find gold."*

### **UMBLE**
The origin of **"humble pie."** Servants in medieval England made pies from "umbles" — deer offal — while nobles ate prime cuts. Eating umble pie meant eating servant food. The word carries hidden history about **making something delicious from what's been dismissed as unworthy**. Reclaiming it is subversive.

# What the App Is

You open your fridge. There's half an onion, some eggs, a sad pepper, and leftover rice from two days ago. The usual response is either "I have nothing" or "guess I'm ordering food."

This app says: actually, you have dinner.

---

## The Core

You take a picture of what you have — your fridge, your counter, whatever's left. The app recognizes the ingredients and shows you what you can actually make with them. Not recipes that require a grocery run. Not aspirational cooking. Just: here's what's possible with exactly what's in front of you.

The recognition happens entirely on your phone. No internet required. You could be in a dorm with terrible WiFi, on a train, wherever — it still works. The AI runs locally, which means it's fast and private. Your sad fridge contents aren't being uploaded anywhere.

When the app isn't sure what something is (is that a zucchini or a cucumber?), it asks you instead of guessing wrong. It's confidence-aware — it knows what it knows and admits what it doesn't.

---

## Who It's Actually For

Students with $30 for groceries this week. Home cooks who are tired and don't want to think. Anyone who's ever stared at random ingredients and felt defeated. People who care about not wasting food but don't have time to be precious about it.

The app assumes realistic constraints: limited money, limited ingredients, limited time, limited energy. It meets you where you are instead of where a cookbook thinks you should be.

---

## The Deeper Thing

There's a quiet philosophy underneath: **what you have is enough**.

Not in a preachy way. Just — the ingredients you already own aren't a limitation, they're a starting point. The app reframes the half-empty fridge from a problem into a prompt. You're not failing at having groceries. You're three ingredients away from a real meal that you made yourself.

This is also about food waste, but framed through action rather than guilt. The pepper going soft in your crisper drawer becomes tonight's dinner instead of tomorrow's trash. Over time, you start to see what you have differently. That's the behavior change — not lecturing about sustainability, but making the sustainable choice the obvious and easy one.

---

## Learning Without Lecturing

Tap any ingredient and you can see what it actually gives you — nutritionally, but also storage tips, what it pairs well with, how different cultures use it. This isn't a nutrition app that makes you feel bad. It's more like: "oh, that's interesting" while you're already cooking.

The same ingredient might show up in a Mexican breakfast, an Italian frittata, or a Korean fried rice. The app understands that food is cultural, not just fuel.

---

## Making It Stick (The Game Layer)

Here's the problem with "good choices": they're easy to abandon. You download a healthy eating app, use it twice, forget it exists.

So there's a light game layer — not intrusive, but present. You complete small quests: "Use something that's been in your fridge for 3+ days." "Cook three meals this week under $5 total ingredients." "Try a recipe from a cuisine you've never made." You earn badges. Some are permanent, some are limited-time events (seasonal ingredients, challenges).

Think Apple Fitness rings or Duolingo streaks, but for cooking. The goal isn't to make you obsessive — it's to make the app something you *want* to open. The healthy, sustainable, budget-friendly choice becomes the one that also feels rewarding.

---

## Make Something Now

Sometimes you don't want to browse. You're hungry, you're tired, you have twelve minutes. There's a mode for that — scan, and the app immediately prioritizes the fastest thing you can make with what you have. No scrolling. One suggestion. Go.

This is the student-at-11pm mode. The I-have-a-meeting-in-30-minutes mode. It respects that sometimes you need the app to just *decide*.

---

## The Community Angle

You're not cooking alone. The app can connect to broader challenges — your university trying to reduce dining hall waste, a residence hall competing to cook the most meals from leftovers, shared recipe discoveries from people with similar ingredients.

This isn't social media. It's more like: other people are doing this too, and that makes it easier. A student sees that 400 other people at their school made something this week instead of throwing food away. That's quietly powerful.

---

## The Technical Bit (Simply)

The ingredient recognition uses machine learning models that run directly on your device — the same type of technology that lets your phone recognize faces in photos, but trained on food. Because it runs locally, it's fast (no waiting for servers) and works offline (no internet dependency). The app gets smarter over time as models improve, but your data stays yours.

The recipe matching is also on-device. You're not searching a remote database — the knowledge lives on your phone. This matters for accessibility: the app works the same whether you have perfect 5G or no connection at all.

---

## What It's Not

It's not a meal planning app that tells you what to buy. It's not a diet app that judges your choices. It's not a social platform where you perform your cooking for likes. It's not a recipe database you have to search through.

It's a tool that looks at what you already have and shows you what's possible. Fast, private, offline, and designed for how people actually live.
